Speech in Nantucket

During an anti-slavery meeting in black episcopal Methodist Zion chapel of New Bedford, Frederick Douglass was explaining his experience as a slave. Wiliam C. Coffin was so impressed with Douglass’s oratorical ability that he invited Douglass to visit the island and be a guest at Nantucket’s first Anti-Slavery Convention

During the Nantucket Convention

In the evening of the second day of the convention, Frederick Douglass was urged to give a speech. Douglass was nervous and described it as a “Severe Cross” which he “took up reluctantly” In his autobigraphy he wrote, “The truth was, I felt myself a slave, and the idea of speaking to white people weighed me down.” While Douglass had trembled with nerves over the course of his speech, one man who witnessed the event noted that Douglass spoke with “Such intellectual power-wisdom as well as wit-that all present were astonished”

Douglass and The Women's Right to Vote

Douglass had earnestly supported womens rights as well. As he states in his auto biography:

          In respect to political rights, we hold woman to be justly entitled to all we claim for man. We go farther, and express our conviction that all political rights which it is expedient for man to exercise, it is equally so for women. All that distinguishes man as an intelligent and accountable being, is equally true of woman; and if that government is only just which governs by the free consent of the governed, there can be no reason in the world for denying to woman the exercise of the elective franchise, or a hand in making and administering the laws of the land. Our doctrine is, that “Right is of no sex.”
